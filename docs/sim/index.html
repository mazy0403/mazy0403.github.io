Simplified Meta-Transfer

January 22, 2024
Academe

title: Simplified Meta-Transfer to Improve Exploration of Reinforcement Learning

abstract: Reinforcement learning (RL) agents are suffering from the reward sparsity in complex environments, especially for autonomous driving caused by corner scenarios and uncertain conditions. The reason is that complex environments feature the presence of severe reward hackings and mixed sparse rewards. In this paper, we propose a Simplified Meta-Transfer (SimMT) method, which uses two floating-point numbers as conservative and aggressive exploration factors and constructs meta-transfer information, respectively. The states are further augmented separately and competence-based unsupervised reinforcement learning (URL) with further compensation for intrinsic motivation drives the agents to explore the environment. The proposed method is analyzed under various traffic flow conditions, and the effect is improved by more than 45% compared to the baseline. Currently SimMT realizes SOTA in the field of RL for autonomous driving decision making.

We also delve into the difficulties of reinforcement learning methods in the field of autonomous driving in this study.

Accessibility and feasible domain correlation lead to experience aggregation.

For a novice, it is difficult to survive in a dangerous environment. For an agent of autonomous driving, survival game, it is exposed to the risk of death. If the feasible domain space is divided according to progressive units, such as time, location. Then according to this progressive unit as the independent variable, the accessibility of the corresponding feasible policy is diminished. This will lead to exploration experiences that will be heavily weighted towards the starting point. This has a limited impact on exploration methods where both intrinsic motivation and external supervision play a role. However it will result in uneven exploration experience for self-supervised exploration methods represented by URLs (in Fig. 2).

Complex environments with multiple combinations of reward items lead to mixed reward sparsity.

The complexity of complex environments often stems from additional expectations. Distinguished from ATARI’s clearance$\backslash$score, the robot’s behavioral goal, complex environments such as traffic flow often have a large number of additional expectations. For example, autonomous driving needs to satisfy: (1) getting from A to B, (2) safety, (3) meeting traffic regulations, (4) comfort, (5) speed, (6) gas mileage, and so on. These excessive expectations lead to a complex combination of various reward components. Exploratory methods are known to be a good way to cope with the phenomenon of reward sparsity. However, there are a large number of reward programs in complex environments, including sparse and continuous rewards, as shown in Fig. 3. This type of reward can mask sparse rewards, yet the environment does suffer from sparse rewards.

**Complex environments lead to substantial reward hackings. **

According to this complex combination of rewards mentioned in, they on the other hand lead to a large number of reward hackings . A large number of reward-hacking policies constitute a hacking space that becomes a distortion within the feasible domain of autonomous driving. Often a large amount of comprehensive a priori knowledge is required to avoid falling into the hacking space. The impact of this is disastrous for exploration algorithms where intrinsic motivation and external supervision work simultaneously, as they are unable to provide a large amount of knowledge at the same moment.

Methods in complex environments are more sensitive to the noise effect of additional information.

The reason why environments such as traffic flows are considered extremely complex for RL is that the complexity of their action combinations. The most iconic policy of autonomous driving is continuous lane changing, which can be utilized to drive a car from Shanghai to Vienna. Unlike the complexity of the strategies, the actions of their agents themselves are not complex. Such as self-driving vehicles, there are only two actions, steering wheel angle and gas pedal depth. By combining simple actions into complex strategies, this leads to RL agents that are extremely sensitive to additional information.

The performance of decision-making in autonomous driving is often affected by edge cases and uncertain scenarios. Reinforcement learning (RL)-based approaches, while improving the situation, tend to suffer from reward sparsity, resulting in insufficient experience acquired during the exploration phase. Through experiments, we identified two particular problems with the autonomous driving environment: first, reward hacking causes certain completely ineffective strategies to behave sub-optimally in the space of feasible domains; and second, the mixed sparse reward problem. The two factors augment the state separately, and this study utilizes the mutual information of the two augmented states for potential information discovery and uses their distance measure as an intrinsic motivation to drive the agents to explore the environment. Our approach is analyzed in comparison to typical curiosity mechanisms and RL methods in the presence of traffic flow conditions. The SimMT approach was effective in enriching the exploration experience and learning more effective strategies. Compared to reinforcement learning, SimMT decision-making performance is improved by more than 45%, and training speed is improved by more than 100%. Compared to other RL exploration and decision-making methods, SimMT realizes SOTA in the autonomous driving field. Our code is at: \textit{https://github.com/TJHuangteam/url-gym.
